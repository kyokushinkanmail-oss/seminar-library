Introduction — The Purpose of This Seminar

This seminar approaches everything from the fundamentals. Basic training (kihon) is the very first thing you learn when you start karate. Understanding how those basics are actually used in real kumite — and how to apply the underlying ideas — is, in itself, a weapon.

Devastatingly effective at close range. Force transmits easily — it's "almost like a tackle," delivering overwhelming power, especially with the elbow.
That is the theme of this session: the elbow and the knee.

Chapter 1 — Why Elbows and Knees, Why Now

Long-range kumite is essential — but

In modern kumite, being able to fight at a proper distance is obviously the foundation. But no matter how carefully you manage distance, close-range combat cannot be avoided.

The reason is simple: almost every fighter, when they are in kumite, wants to move forward.

Why does everyone want to advance?

Because "moving forward" looks like the dominant position.

The real motivation

  • You want to win the match
  • You want to look dominant → you want to move forward
  • Both fighters feel this way, so the distance collapses to the extreme

In other words, close-range combat is unavoidable in kumite. This is a structural inevitability.

Chapter 2 — The Structure of Close-Range Combat

Conclusion

  1. Once you are in close, create a situation where your opponent wants to get out of there
  2. As a result, the opponent retreats and you regain distance
  3. This also swings the judges' impression in your favor
  4. The weapons that make this happen are the knee and the elbow

Can you make the opponent feel "I don't want to be here" when the fight closes in? That determines who controls close-range combat.

Chapter 3 — Understanding Distance

Closing the distance is easier than creating it

Trying to back off and retreat is harder — both physically and in terms of timing — than pressing forward into close range. That is why the closure keeps happening.

The optimal punching distance is farther than you think

The distance at which the seiken-zuki (straight punch) delivers maximum power is slightly farther away than most people imagine. Meanwhile, the close range seen in kumite is usually even closer than the full extension of your arm.

In this close range zone, long-range attacks like punches and kicks can't extend fully and struggle to generate power.
→ Short-range attacks — the elbow and the knee — are the weapons that can deliver force here.

Chapter 4 — The Elbow: Condensed Destructive Power

Elbow basics

  • Strike with the point of the elbow (chuto) — the sharp bone tip
  • Target primarily the sternum (danchu) or the chest
  • Landing that sharp point at full force is extraordinarily painful — occasionally fatal. That is the scale of its power.
Handle with extreme care — in training and in matches. The power is so high that only once you can control it does it become a weapon.

How to land on the elbow point

The key: as you throw the elbow, rotate the hand inward. This naturally pushes the point of the elbow to the front.

Training progression

  • Place the point of the elbow on the floor (build the tactile awareness)
  • Press it against the mitt
  • Gradually begin striking

And above all — position is absolutely critical.

Landing conditions and methods

Conditions for striking the danchu or chest:

  • Mirror stance (ai-yotsu) is essential
  • Compared to the fist, the elbow has fewer joints and less angular adjustment
  • Therefore, the positional relationship between you and the opponent becomes decisive

Striking patterns — angle and the opponent's rear hand

  • Angle — drive the elbow into the front of the opponent's torso
  • The rear hand is in the way — without preparation, it will block the strike
  • The fix — throw another attack first so the opponent parries "from the outside," moving their rear hand out of the path

In other words, don't go for the elbow as a single strike. Set up the opponent's hand position first, then thread the elbow in.

Chapter 5 — The Knee: The Strongest Kick at Close Range

The knee is important enough to change the outcome of a match. The strongest kick at close range — almost a tackle.

Striking surfaces

  • The upper inside of the knee
  • The very top of the shinbone (tibia)

How to land the knee

Pattern 1 — Striking with the upper inside of the knee

  • With the rear leg in open stance (kenka-yotsu), drive the knee straight into the opponent's torso
  • You can also throw it straight from a mirror stance

Pattern 2 — Striking with the top of the shinbone

  • Like a basic front kick — push the hips forward and kick
  • From this form, you can fire the knee at any timing of your choosing — that is its great strength
※ Always connect the knee to follow-up. The knee landing is not the end — it's the move that sets up what comes next.

Chapter 6 — Creating Distance from Elbow and Knee

Because the elbow and knee are high-power attacks, when they land, the pain and pressure cause the opponent to give you back a little space. That is your chance to connect combinations.

Example patterns

Elbow → chudan roundhouse kick (the elbow drops the guard, opening the midsection to the kick)

Knee → front kick (the knee creates space, and the front kick takes over the opened gap)

Knee → elbow (stack two high-power strikes back to back)

Summary

  1. Close range will always happen during kumite
  2. Carry high-power weapons precisely so you can regain distance
  3. When you're in close, make the opponent feel "I don't want to be here"
  4. The opponent retreats — and you win the judges' impression decisively
  5. Practice the elbow strike and the knee strike!
